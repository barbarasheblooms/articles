ICP vs. target market: why the distinction will save you months

By SheBlooms 6 min read Stage 1 · Task #7

A target market is a category. An ICP is a specific person you can picture, find, and call. Most early-stage founders have the first but not the second — and it's costing them months of building in the wrong direction.

Ask most founders who their customer is and you'll get an answer that sounds like a market segment: "female entrepreneurs," "small business owners," "HR managers at mid-size companies." These answers aren't wrong, but they're not useful. They describe a category of people, not the specific person whose problem is so acute that they'll pay you to solve it today.

The Ideal Customer Profile isn't a marketing concept. It's a validation tool. It tells you who to interview, what to build first, and how to talk about it. Getting it right at Stage 1 is one of the highest-leverage things you can do.

Target market vs. ICP: the actual difference

Target market (too broad)
  • Female founders in Europe
  • HR professionals
  • SME owners
  • Gen Z consumers
  • E-commerce businesses
ICP (specific enough to act on)
  • First-time female founders, pre-revenue, building B2B SaaS, based in Iberia, working solo or with one co-founder
  • HR managers at 50–200 person tech companies, responsible for hiring engineering roles, experiencing >90-day time-to-hire

The test for whether you have an ICP or a target market is simple: could you find 50 specific people who match your description on LinkedIn in under an hour? If not, you're still at target market level. An ICP is specific enough to generate a list.

Why being too broad hurts you

When your ICP is too broad, three things go wrong simultaneously.

Your interviews give you contradictory data. If "female founders" is your ICP, you'll interview solo pre-revenue founders and Series A founders with teams of 20, and they'll give you completely different answers. You'll try to reconcile them into a single insight and end up with something true for no one.

Your product tries to serve everyone. Without a tight ICP, every feature request seems valid. You build for the loudest user, not the most representative one. The product grows in all directions and becomes excellent at nothing.

Your messaging reaches no one. "Built for entrepreneurs" resonates with no one. "Built for pre-revenue female founders who are preparing for their first investor meeting" resonates precisely with the person who needs it most and who will share it with everyone she knows who's in the same situation.

How to narrow your ICP without guessing

The ICP isn't something you decide — it's something you discover. Your job at Stage 1 is to interview enough people that the pattern of who feels the problem most acutely becomes visible. Then you build for that person first.

Start broad: "female founders in Europe" ↓
Add stage: "pre-revenue female founders" ↓
Add context: "pre-revenue female founders building B2B products" ↓
Add trigger: "...who have decided to raise their first round in the next 12 months" ↓
Your ICP: "First-time female founders, pre-revenue, B2B, decided to raise, no existing investor relationships"

Each layer of specificity comes from your interviews. If you find that the pain is consistent across B2B and B2C founders, don't add that filter. If you find that founders who've already decided to raise feel the pain three times more acutely than those who are still considering it, add the trigger.

The ICP definition test

Before you commit to an ICP, run it through these four questions:

  1. Can I find them? Can I search for this person on LinkedIn, in a community, or at an event? If not, the definition is too abstract.
  2. Do they have the problem I'm solving? Not "might have" — do they demonstrably, consistently have it? From interviews, not assumption.
  3. Do they have the ability and motivation to pay? The most painful problem means nothing if the person who has it can't or won't pay to solve it.
  4. Are there enough of them? Your ICP needs to be specific, but not so specific that the total addressable market is 200 people. As a rough check: can you find 1,000 people who match this profile in Europe? If yes, you're fine.
Example: narrowing from target market to ICP
Target market: Women in business who need financial support.

First pass ICP: Female founders seeking their first round of funding.

Validated ICP (after 20 interviews): First-time female founders in Southern Europe, pre-revenue to €5K MRR, building B2B SaaS or marketplace, who have validated their problem but lack investor relationships and don't know how to translate their work into investor-grade evidence.

The difference: the second version emerged from interviews. Fourteen of twenty people interviewed fit this profile. The other six had different problems with different urgency. The fourteen became the ICP.

"I spent three months trying to build for 'female founders.' It wasn't until I admitted I was really building for one very specific type of founder — the one I used to be — that everything clicked."

The ICP is a starting point, not a constraint

A tight ICP doesn't mean you'll only ever serve one type of customer. It means you'll serve the first one so well that they tell everyone else about you, and you'll understand the problem so deeply that expanding is a deliberate choice, not a scramble.

Every company that scaled broadly started narrow. Airbnb started with air mattresses in San Francisco. Stripe started with developers. Facebook started with Harvard. The ICP is where you go deep before you go wide.

A practical prompt

Write a description of your ICP that starts with: "She is a [role] at [type of company] who [specific situation] and is currently [trigger event] because [root cause]." If you can't complete that sentence with specifics from your interviews, you need more interviews.

Key takeaways
  • A target market is a category. An ICP is a specific person you can find on LinkedIn in under an hour.
  • Being too broad produces contradictory interview data, unfocused products, and messaging that resonates with no one.
  • Narrow your ICP by adding layers of specificity based on what you hear in interviews — not by deciding upfront.
  • Test your ICP against four questions: can I find them, do they have the problem, can they pay, are there enough of them?
  • Start narrow. Go deep. Expand when you're ready.
