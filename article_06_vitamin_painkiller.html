5 signs you're solving a vitamin problem, not a painkiller

Guest perspective · Operator & early-stage investor 7 min read

Vitamins are good. People know they should take them. They buy them enthusiastically, use them for two weeks, and forget about them. If your product is a vitamin, your customers will do the same thing.

The vitamin vs. painkiller framework is one of the most cited in startup circles, and also one of the most misapplied. Founders hear it, nod, declare that they're building a painkiller, and move on. The problem is that vitamins rarely announce themselves.

Vitamins feel like painkillers to the person building them. The difference is almost always invisible from the inside. It only becomes clear from the outside — from interview data, from churn, from the moment you look at your retention curve and ask yourself why it looks the way it does.

Here are the five signs that your problem might be a vitamin, what they actually mean, and what you can do about each one.

1
People agree it's a problem but can't remember the last time it happened

This is the most common vitamin tell in problem interviews. You describe the problem. They agree immediately. You feel validated. Then you ask: "When was the last time this happened to you?" And they pause. And think. And eventually say something like "probably last year" or "I'm sure it comes up sometimes."

Problems that are hard to recall are not urgent. Urgency requires recency. The problems people will pay to solve are the ones sitting in their inbox right now, not the ones they vaguely remember as being occasionally annoying.

The tell:
Agreement is immediate. Recall is slow. No specific example without prompting.
What to do:
Ask for the specific last instance in every interview. If fewer than 70% can give you one from the last 30 days, reconsider the urgency of the problem.
2
Nobody has built a workaround

When a problem is genuinely painful, people don't wait for a perfect solution. They cobble one together. They build a spreadsheet. They hire a part-time contractor. They use three different tools and manually combine the outputs. They create a Notion template that only they understand. The workaround is evidence of pain — it's proof that someone cared enough to spend time fixing something that was broken.

When nobody has a workaround, it usually means one of two things: either the problem doesn't hurt badly enough to justify the effort of improvising a solution, or the problem is too abstract to have a practical dimension at all.

The tell:
You ask "what do you do today to deal with this?" and the answer is "nothing, really" or "I just accept it."
What to do:
A missing workaround isn't always a red flag — sometimes the problem is so new that no workaround exists yet. But you need a different form of evidence: are they actively looking for a solution? Have they tried anything that failed? Absence of workaround requires more evidence of intent, not less.
3
The enthusiastic responses come mostly from your network

Early feedback from your personal network is almost always more positive than feedback from strangers. People who know you want you to succeed. They frame their hesitations as encouragement. They focus on the upside. And because you've selected them, they often share your values and your worldview — which means they may share your sense that a problem exists even when most of the market doesn't feel it.

A strong network effect in your early validation data is one of the clearest vitamin signals there is. It means you're measuring enthusiasm for you, not for the problem.

The tell:
Your best feedback comes from people who introduced themselves as supporters before they gave their opinion. Strangers are less enthusiastic than people in your circle.
What to do:
Run 10 interviews with complete strangers who match your ICP. No warm intros, no community connections, no one who has heard of you. If the enthusiasm drops significantly, you have a network problem, not a market validation.
4
People love the idea but nobody asks "when can I use it?"

There is a specific type of interview response that feels like validation but is actually a vitamin signal: enthusiastic engagement with the concept combined with zero urgency about accessing the product. "This is great, I'd love to see where this goes" is not a buying signal. "When can I sign up? Is there a waitlist?" is a buying signal.

Painkillers create demand before they exist. People refer others to them before they launch. Vitamins generate intellectual interest and then get forgotten.

The tell:
You leave the interview feeling great. There was energy, interest, positive feedback. But nobody asked how to get access. Nobody asked about price. Nobody volunteered to be a beta user.
What to do:
At the end of each interview, say: "We're working on a solution for this. Would you want to be among the first to try it?" If fewer than 50% say yes immediately and without qualifications, the problem isn't urgent enough. If they say yes but never follow up when you send the link, same conclusion.
5
Early users engage once and don't come back

Sometimes the vitamin signal doesn't appear in validation — it appears in retention. If you have an MVP or early product and your Week 2 retention is below 15%, the most common explanation isn't onboarding failure or product bugs. It's a vitamin problem: users found what they needed, the pain wasn't recurring, and they have no reason to return.

This is the hardest vitamin to fix because you've already built something. The question to ask isn't "how do we improve retention?" — it's "what would make this problem recur frequently enough that our users have a reason to come back?"

The tell:
High initial activation, low Week 2 retention. Users say they found it useful but "haven't needed it again yet." Low organic referrals even from users who liked it.
What to do:
Identify the recurring trigger — the regular event or context that would bring users back. If no recurring trigger exists, consider whether your product needs a different use case, a different user segment, or a subscription mechanism that creates artificial recurrence.

What to do if you're a vitamin

Most products that start as vitamins don't pivot to being painkillers — they find a painkiller use case adjacent to the vitamin one. The core technology or insight is often the same. What changes is the user, the context, and the framing.

The question to ask isn't "how do I make people need this more?" It's "who has a version of this problem that they cannot afford to ignore?" That person exists. Your job is to find them.

"We were building a nice-to-have for most of our users. But 20% of them were using it daily and would have paid 10x what we were charging. We stopped trying to convert the 80% and started building everything for the 20%."

The honest question to ask yourself

If your product disappeared tomorrow, what would your users do? If the answer is "they'd find something else quickly" or "they'd probably be fine," you're a vitamin. If the answer is "they'd be genuinely stuck and would come looking for you," you're a painkiller. The goal of Stage 1 is to build toward the second answer.

Key takeaways
  • Vitamins feel like painkillers to the person building them. The difference only shows up in data.
  • The five signs: no specific recall, no workarounds, network-skewed enthusiasm, interest without urgency, single-use retention.
  • A vitamin problem isn't a dead end — it's often a sign you're solving for the wrong user segment or the wrong trigger event.
  • Find the users for whom this is a painkiller. They usually exist — they're just not who you initially thought they were.
  • The test: if your product disappeared tomorrow, would your users be stuck or just mildly inconvenienced?
