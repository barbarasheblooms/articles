What "70% pain severity" actually means — and how to measure it

By SheBlooms 6 min read Stage 1 · Gate task

The Stage 1 gate requires that 70% of your interviewees show high pain severity. This isn't a number you feel — it's a number you measure. Here's exactly what it means and how to track it.

The 70% pain severity threshold is one of the most misunderstood requirements in Stage 1. Founders either treat it as a box to tick ("yes, lots of people have this problem") or they dismiss it as arbitrary. It's neither. It's a precise signal designed to answer a very specific question: is this problem painful enough that people will actually change their behaviour to solve it?

That question matters because most problems are real but not urgent. People acknowledge them, complain about them, and then do nothing about them. Building a product for a problem that doesn't drive behaviour change is one of the most common reasons early startups stall.

Pain severity is three things, not one

The mistake most founders make is measuring pain as a single dimension — "how much does this bother them?" But pain severity is actually the intersection of three independent signals:

Dimension
What you're measuring
Low signal (1–2)
Not investable
High signal (4–5)
Investable
Frequency
How often does this happen?
Weekly? Quarterly? Once a year?
"It comes up occasionally" / can't recall last instance
"It happens every time I try to do X" / "At least once a week"
Impact
How much does it cost?
Time, money, opportunity, emotion
"It's a bit annoying" / no concrete cost named
"It costs me 4 hours a week" / "We lost a client because of this"
Urgency
How motivated are they to fix it now?
Would they act today if a solution existed?
"I'd probably try it someday" / passive interest
"I've been looking for something like this for months" / asked "when can I use it?"

To count as "high pain severity," an interviewee needs to show strong signals in at least two of the three dimensions — and the impact dimension is non-negotiable. An annoying problem that happens every day but costs nothing is still not investable.

Where the 70% comes from

70%
The threshold and why it matters
If fewer than 70% of your ICP interviewees show high pain severity, you have two possibilities: the problem isn't urgent enough for most people, or your ICP definition is too broad and includes people who have the problem lightly alongside those who have it acutely. Either way, you need to keep going — more interviews, tighter ICP, or a different problem framing.

The 70% isn't a magic number. It's a practical threshold that distinguishes "a problem that exists" from "a problem that drives action." Below 70%, you're likely building for the enthusiastic minority while the majority works around you or ignores you.

How to score it in practice

In each problem interview, score three dimensions on a 1–5 scale. An interviewee counts as "high severity" if their combined score is 10 or above (out of 15) and their impact score is 4 or 5.

In this sample of 6, 3 interviewees score high — that's 50%. Below threshold. But notice the pattern: interviews 3 and 6 are post-revenue and Series A founders respectively. If the ICP is "pre-revenue, first-time founder," these two may be outside the target. Remove them, and 3 of 4 = 75%. Above threshold.

This is why ICP definition and pain severity measurement are inseparable. Your score is only as meaningful as the quality of your sample.

What to do if you're at 50%

A 50% pain severity rate is one of the most useful results you can get, because it tells you something specific: the problem is real for some people, but not all people in your current sample. Your job is to figure out what the high-severity group has in common.

  • Look at the high-scorers: what's their stage, role, industry, situation?
  • Look at the low-scorers: what's different about them?
  • Reformulate your ICP to exclude the low-severity profiles.
  • Run the next 10 interviews targeting only the high-severity profile.

If you do this and the rate climbs to 70%+, you've found your real ICP. If it stays flat regardless of who you interview, the problem may genuinely not be urgent enough — and that's a different conversation about whether to pivot.

"We were at 55% and getting frustrated. Then we noticed all our high-severity interviews were with founders who had already tried to raise and been rejected. We stopped interviewing founders who hadn't tried. The rate went to 82%."

Tracking tip

Keep a running tally as you go: total interviews, high-severity count, running percentage. You don't need to wait until interview 20 to check your rate. If you're at 8 interviews and only 2 are high-severity, that's 25% — an early signal to revisit your ICP before going further.

Key takeaways
  • Pain severity is three dimensions: frequency, impact, and urgency. Impact is non-negotiable.
  • Score each interview. An interviewee counts as "high severity" if they score 10+/15 with an impact score of 4–5.
  • 70%+ of your ICP interviews must be high severity to pass the Stage 1 gate.
  • If you're at 50%, the pattern of who scores high is more valuable than the average.
  • ICP definition and pain severity are inseparable — your rate is only valid for a well-defined ICP.
