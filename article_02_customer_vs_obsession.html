The difference between a customer problem and a founder obsession

By SheBlooms 7 min read Stage 1 · Foundation

You built something you believed in. That's not a bug — it's what got you here. But somewhere between belief and building, many founders stop solving the customer's problem and start solving their own. Here's how to tell the difference.

The most seductive trap in early-stage entrepreneurship isn't building the wrong product. It's building the right product for yourself, not for the market.

Founder obsessions are powerful. They drive the relentless energy that early companies run on. The problem is that they can be completely disconnected from what customers actually need — and by the time you find out, you've spent months building something nobody will pay for.

Understanding the difference is Stage 1's most important skill, and the one most rarely taught.

What a customer problem looks like

A customer problem exists independent of you. It existed before you arrived, it will exist if you disappear, and the people who have it are already trying (however badly) to solve it. They have workarounds. They have frustrations. They have numbers they could tell you — hours lost, money spent, opportunities missed.

A customer problem also has a natural owner: the person who has it feels it as their problem, not as an abstract inconvenience that affects someone else. When you ask them about it, they don't need to think. They lean forward.

What a founder obsession looks like

A founder obsession is something you care deeply about, often for very good reasons. You've experienced it. You've read about it. You see it as a systemic failure that affects many people. The injustice of it bothers you.

The problem is that deep personal conviction can masquerade as customer insight. You believe so strongly that the problem is real that you stop checking whether other people feel it the way you do — with the same intensity, the same urgency, the same willingness to act.

Founder obsessions often produce beautiful products that serve almost no one, or that serve only the specific type of person the founder is.

Signs of a founder obsession Signs of a customer problem
You have to explain why this is a problem before they agree it's a problem They describe the problem before you ask about it
Your interviewees agree it exists but can't remember the last time it affected them They can tell you exactly when it last happened and how much it cost them
The people who care most about it are like you — same background, same values, same frustrations A wide range of people with different backgrounds all feel it similarly
No one has a workaround because they don't need one Everyone has a different workaround because nothing good exists yet
When you stop talking about it, the energy in the room drops When you ask about it, the energy in the room rises

The three tests

Apply these three tests to your problem hypothesis:
Test 1: The stranger test
Can a stranger, unprompted, describe this problem back to you in their own words within 60 seconds of you asking about their experience in this area?
Fail signal: They need you to explain it. They agree once you've framed it, but they wouldn't have raised it themselves.
Pass signal: They start talking before you finish the question. They have specifics. You struggle to get a word in.
Test 2: The last-time test
Ask: "When was the last time this happened to you?" and "What did you do about it?"
Fail signal: They struggle to remember a specific instance. They give you a general impression. They haven't done anything about it.
Pass signal: They remember it clearly. They have context, numbers, frustration. They built a workaround or paid someone to fix it.
Test 3: The removal test
Ask: "If this problem disappeared tomorrow, what would change for you?"
Fail signal: The answer is vague — "it would be nice," "things would be easier." There's no concrete outcome on the other side.
Pass signal: The answer is immediate and specific — "I'd save eight hours a week," "I'd stop losing clients at this stage," "I'd finally be able to focus on growth."

The trap of the mission-driven founder

This distinction is particularly difficult for mission-driven founders — and most SheBlooms founders are mission-driven. When you care deeply about a cause (gender equity, access to capital, financial literacy), it can be hard to separate your mission from your customer's problem.

Your mission is why you're doing this. Your customer's problem is what you're building. They are not the same thing.

A founder building for female entrepreneurs because she believes in gender equity is mission-driven. But the product she builds still needs to solve a specific, felt, urgent problem that real customers will pay to resolve. "Supporting female founders" is a mission. "Helping female founders at the pre-seed stage build investor-grade evidence of demand" is a problem.

"I thought I understood the problem because I'd lived it. What I'd lived was one version of it. My customers were living six different versions, and the one I'd built for was the smallest."

What to do if you suspect you have an obsession, not a problem

The good news: founder obsessions don't disqualify you. They often point toward a real problem — just not the exact one you're imagining. The path forward is almost always to interview more, listen more carefully, and be willing to let the problem statement change.

Specifically:

  • Run 20+ interviews with strangers who match your ICP. Not people who know you, admire you, or share your values.
  • Actively look for disconfirmation. After each interview, write down the one thing that challenged your assumption.
  • Track how often people raise the problem unprompted versus how often you had to introduce it.
  • If the ratio is below 50% (you're framing it more often than they raise it), revisit the problem definition.
The reframe that helps

Stop asking "is this a real problem?" and start asking "is this a problem that this specific person, right now, would pay to solve?" The first question invites philosophical agreement. The second question requires evidence.

Key takeaways
  • A customer problem exists independently of you. A founder obsession exists because of you.
  • The difference shows up in interviews: customer problems get raised unprompted; obsessions need framing.
  • Apply the three tests: stranger test, last-time test, removal test.
  • Mission-driven founders are especially at risk — your mission is why you build, not what you build.
  • Obsessions don't disqualify you. They often point toward the real problem. Keep interviewing and keep listening.
