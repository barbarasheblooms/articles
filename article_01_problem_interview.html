How to run a problem interview that actually gives you data

By SheBlooms 8 min read Stage 1 · Task #3

Most founders come out of problem interviews more convinced than when they went in. That's a red flag, not a green one. Here's how to run interviews that challenge your assumptions instead of confirming them.

The problem interview is the most important thing you'll do at Stage 1 — and the most commonly done wrong. Founders leave feeling validated. They quote enthusiastic responses. They say "everyone I spoke to had this problem." And then they build something nobody uses.

The issue isn't that they spoke to the wrong people. It's that they asked the wrong questions, listened for the wrong signals, and walked away with stories instead of data.

Here's what actually works.

Before you start: the one rule that changes everything

Do not mention your solution. Not once. Not even as a hint. Not as "we're exploring something that might help with this." The moment you describe your product, the interview becomes a pitch evaluation, and the person in front of you becomes a polite audience trying not to hurt your feelings.

You are there to understand their world. You are not there to sell them anything — not even an idea.

This is harder than it sounds. You've been thinking about your solution for weeks. It's sitting right there. When they describe a pain that your product solves perfectly, every instinct tells you to say something. Don't. Take the note and keep asking questions.

Who to interview — and who not to

The most common sampling error: interviewing people who are easy to reach, not people who match your ICP. Friends, family, former colleagues, people in your network who are "kind of" in the target — these are the wrong interviewees. They'll be polite. They'll want to support you. They'll say "yes, that's a problem" because they like you.

You need strangers. Specifically, strangers who match the profile you've defined as your Ideal Customer Profile. If you haven't defined your ICP yet, that's your first task — you can't run useful problem interviews without it.

The Stage 1 gate requires 20+ interviews. That number exists because 5–10 interviews give you stories. Twenty or more start giving you patterns. And patterns are the only thing you can build on.

The five questions that actually work

A good problem interview is a conversation, not a questionnaire. But it has a spine. These five questions, in roughly this order, will get you what you need:

1
"Tell me about your day-to-day in [relevant area]."
Open wide. You're building context. You're letting them talk without leading them anywhere. Listen for the areas where they slow down, use stronger language, or sigh.
2
"What's the hardest part of [area]?"
This is the first filter. If they struggle to answer — if the problem doesn't come out naturally — that's a data point. If they light up and have three things to say, that's a different data point.
3
"Walk me through the last time that happened."
This is the most important question in the interview. You're asking for a story, not an opinion. Stories are factual. Opinions are aspirational. "I'd probably want something that does X" tells you nothing. "Last Tuesday I spent three hours doing Y because there was no other way" tells you everything.
4
"What do you do today to deal with it?"
This reveals the competitive landscape better than any market research. It tells you what they're willing to pay (in time or money) to solve the problem right now. It also tells you how deeply the pain is felt — people build elaborate workarounds for things that really hurt them.
5
"If this problem disappeared tomorrow, what would change?"
This reveals the real value on the other side of the pain. Most founders talk about features. Investors and customers care about outcomes. This question gives you the outcome.

What to listen for — and what to ignore

There are two types of responses that should make you stop and write everything down:

Unprompted specificity. When someone gives you numbers without being asked — "I spend about four hours a week on this," "it costs us roughly €2,000 per client," "I've tried three different tools and none of them worked" — they're telling you the problem is real and felt. Vague answers ("yeah it's sometimes annoying") mean vague pain.

Self-built workarounds. If someone has built a spreadsheet, a Notion template, a manual process, or any kind of improvised solution to the problem, you've found something. People don't build workarounds for problems they don't care about. The worse the workaround, the bigger the opportunity.

And two things to treat as warning signs:

Agreement without evidence. "Yes, that's definitely a problem" in response to a question you framed is not validation. You framed the question. They agreed. Ask them to tell you about a time it happened. If they can't, move on.

Future-tense language. "I would probably use that," "that would be really helpful," "I think people would love this" — all of these are hypothetical. The only thing that matters is what people have done, not what they say they would do.

The pattern you need to see

After 20 interviews, you should be able to answer three questions with evidence:

  1. Can you describe the problem in one sentence, using their language? Not your language — theirs. If you still need jargon or complex framing, you don't understand the problem well enough yet.
  2. Do 70%+ of the people you interviewed experience this problem at a frequency and intensity that would make them pay to fix it? This is the Stage 1 pain severity gate.
  3. Can you name the top three things they currently use to solve it? If you can't describe your competitive landscape from memory after 20 interviews, you were talking to the wrong people or asking the wrong questions.

"I came in thinking I understood the problem. After the first ten interviews, I realised I'd been solving the symptom, not the cause. The interviews didn't validate my idea — they gave me a better one."

— SheBlooms founder, Stage 2

A practical note on scheduling

Twenty interviews sounds like a lot. At two per week, that's ten weeks. At four per week — two short calls per day, two days a week — that's five weeks. Most founders who struggle to reach 20 are booking them inefficiently, not lacking access. Ask every interviewee for one referral at the end of the call. Warm referrals convert at three times the rate of cold outreach.

Common mistake to avoid

Running a group interview or a focus group instead of one-on-ones. Groups create social pressure to agree. The loudest person in the room shapes everyone else's answers. One-on-ones give you 20 independent data points. Groups give you one.

Key takeaways
  • Never mention your solution during a problem interview.
  • Interview strangers who match your ICP, not people who know you.
  • Ask for stories about the last time the problem happened, not opinions about what they'd want.
  • Specificity and self-built workarounds are the strongest signals of real pain.
  • You need 20+ interviews before patterns emerge. Two per week gets you there in ten weeks.
  • The output is three things: a one-sentence problem statement in their language, a pain severity rate, and a competitive landscape.
